## Slide 7: Scenarios (2–3) for Growth & Disruption Outcomes

### Scenario 1: "Network Flywheel Ignites"
**What becomes true:**
- Mayo Clinic deployment proven at scale; 5+ major academic medical centers and 2+ top-20 pharma sponsors adopt within 12 months
- Cross-side network effects kick in: sponsors prefer sites on Mercury, sites prefer sponsors on Mercury
- Metagreement™ data becomes the definitive CTA benchmarking source

**Leading indicators:** Customer count growth >3x YoY; sponsor-side revenue emerging; multi-party negotiations increasing; NPS >60

**Implications for product/AI/GTM:** Invest heavily in sponsor-side features; build API integrations with top CTMS platforms; expand AI playbooks to new agreement types

### Scenario 2: "Solid Niche, Linear Growth"
**What becomes true:**
- Product delivers strong ROI for sites but sponsor adoption is slow
- Network effects remain limited (one-sided adoption)
- Mercury grows steadily but doesn't achieve market-defining position

**Leading indicators:** Site customer growth steady but sponsor pipeline weak; single-side usage dominates; competitive alternatives emerge from CLM vendors

**Implications for product/AI/GTM:** Focus on deepening site-side value; consider white-label / OEM model for sponsors; strengthen partnership channel

### Scenario 3: "AI Commoditization Pressures"
**What becomes true:**
- Frontier AI models (GPT, Claude) enable any CLM vendor to add CTA-specific markup
- Generic contract AI tools become "good enough" for 80% of use cases
- Mercury's domain-specific moat erodes faster than network effects can build

**Leading indicators:** Veeva / Clio / Ironclad announce CTA-specific features; large CROs build internal tools; commoditized AI contract review becomes widespread

**Implications for product/AI/GTM:** Double down on network effects and multi-party collaboration (harder to replicate than AI); accelerate data asset strategy; pursue strategic acquisition to lock in distribution

## Slide 8: Disruption Risk — Executive Summary

### Frontier Model Companies — Risk: Medium
**INFERENCE:** OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google could enable any platform to add contract AI capabilities through APIs. However, they are unlikely to build purpose-built CTA negotiation platforms themselves. Mercury is a consumer of these models (uses all three via direct API). Per management: the pipeline is described as "as important as the LLM model" — a single model call cannot replicate the multi-document, multi-step orchestration that manages 500+ sub-issues across 150 playbook topics. Risk is indirect — frontier models lower the barrier for competitors, not for direct entry.

### Competitors — Risk: Medium-High
**FACTS:** Veeva (SiteVault ecosystem, expanding rapidly in site-centric tools), Florence Healthcare (Mercury's current partner — connected to 37,000+ sites in 90 countries, already in eISF/eRegulatory), Greenphire (site payments), and broader CLM vendors (Ironclad, Agiloft, DocuSign) all have adjacent positions.
**Per management:** Mercury has won every competitive bake-off against generic tools. George Washington University tried Spot Draft, failed, and returned to Mercury. Duke did a side-by-side comparison. Generic tools miss issues or incorrectly mark up domain-specific concepts (e.g., subject injury clauses). Per management: "it never works" when organizations try non-vertically-focused tools because those vendors lack the domain expertise, playbooks, and understanding of how sites interact with AI.
**INFERENCE:** The most credible competitive threat remains Veeva, which has deep clinical trial site relationships and resources. Florence is currently a partner but could become a competitor. The competitive moat is strengthened by the transcript evidence — the pipeline complexity, domain-specific playbook library, and congruency pipeline are genuine barriers.

### Niche / Lightweight Solutions — Risk: Low-Medium
**Per management:** Sites are described as "resource starved, don't have technology resources, don't have AI experts." Mix of lawyers and non-lawyers negotiating contracts. Even large organizations have incomplete playbooks. This makes self-build unlikely for sites. Large CROs could replicate basic AI review internally but per management, the multi-party collaboration and congruency pipeline are harder to productize.

## Slide 12: "Build-It-Today" Replicability

### Column 1: Build in Days (off-the-shelf / low-code)
- Basic AI contract review/redlining using GPT-4 / Claude API + a document upload interface
- Clause-level extraction and comparison against a static playbook
- Simple compliance checklist generation
- **Key point:** The "AI markup" feature in isolation is increasingly table stakes. Per management: customers who tried this approach (e.g., George Washington University with Spot Draft) found it "didn't work" — the generic tool missed half the issues or incorrectly addressed domain-specific concepts.

### Column 2: Build in Weeks-Months
- Multi-party collaboration platform with role-based access (sponsor, site, CRO, counsel)
- Integration with eISF/CTMS platforms (e.g., Florence, SiteVault)
- Structured data extraction (Metagreement™-style) that maintains data across negotiation rounds
- Playbook management system with institutional customization
- Round-by-round change tracking with AI-generated summaries
- **Key point:** Per management, a playbook has ~150 topics with multiple required sub-items each, yielding 500+ distinct sub-issues. Building a pipeline that analyzes all of these holistically in a single pass requires significant prompt engineering, state machine orchestration, and domain expertise.

### Column 3: Hard / Months-Years
- **Multi-document congruency pipeline:** Per management: ingesting a 150-page protocol, aligning it to 6 different manuals, comparing to sponsor budget, comparing to internal rate cards, and performing coverage analysis is a multi-step orchestration challenge. Per management, they have "pushed Claude and GPT as hard as they can go" on context window management for this. The pipeline is described as "as important as the LLM model."
- **Network effects / multi-party trust:** Getting both sides of the negotiation onto one platform requires trust, neutrality, and critical mass — this cannot be built, it must be earned over time
- **Domain-specific historical data:** Per management: graph database tracks clause reuse across organizations ("this clause used in 52 places"), contract network relationships, and version history. Years of production data required for meaningful benchmarking.
- **Institutional playbook library:** Per management: analyst team (4 analysts + 1 director) converts every new customer's offline playbooks to AI-optimized profiles during onboarding. Even large organizations have incomplete playbooks — Mercury fills the gaps with domain expertise. This library compounds with each new customer.
- **AI-native SDLC and engineering culture:** Per management/SDLC diagram: Claude Code as primary coding tool, spec + TDD first approach, specialized sub-agents (Cypher, Review, UI, PR Generation, Agent Builder, Red Green Refactor), Claude review in CI/CD, weekly AI review calls. Replicating this development velocity requires not just tools but cultural adoption.
- **Regulatory trust and compliance track record:** Credibility in regulated clinical research takes years. Per management: 3-5 security questionnaires/month from AMCs and clinical sites.
- **Mayo Clinic collaboration and endorsement:** Institutional credibility from a world-class health system — cannot be replicated
