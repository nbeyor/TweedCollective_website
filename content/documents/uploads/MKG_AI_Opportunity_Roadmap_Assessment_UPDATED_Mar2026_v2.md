# 1. Executive Summary

## Current AI Posture

MKG has done more AI work than most companies its size. Across the ION platform, the organization has stood up 15+ internal tools under the KINETICS banner (workflow optimization) and 7+ client-facing products under DIFFUSION (differentiated analytics and research offerings), all connected to a proprietary data backbone. The governance structure — weekly AI committee, biweekly advocates, monthly senior leadership review, CFO gating on build costs — is genuinely strong for a ~$150M company.

The problem isn't ideas. It's prioritization, measurement, and speed to value. Fifteen branded AI initiatives across $150M of revenue is too much surface area. Several internal tools overlap (four of them touch the same editorial pipeline). Several client-facing products risk being displaced by rapidly improving foundation models unless they're anchored in proprietary data. And MKG has no closed-loop measurement system connecting AI product usage to business outcomes.

**AI maturity level: Upper-Mid / Structured & Scaling** — strong governance and meaningful tooling, but a fragmented portfolio and limited outcome measurement. Total estimated Year 1 AI opportunity: **$5.0M–$13.6M** (3.3–9.1% of revenue), split between internal productivity gains ($3.2M–$6.9M in labor cost equivalent) and external revenue upside ($1.8M–$6.8M in incremental revenue from AI-differentiated products).

## Top Recommendations

1. **Rationalize the AI portfolio.** Consolidate adjacent products — starting with the four editorial pipeline tools — and stop building net-new products until overlap is addressed.
2. **Differentiate through data + medical expertise.** The moat is engagement data, medical judgment, and integrated commercialization services — not AI capability alone.
3. **Instrument ROI with leading indicators.** Adoption, cycle time, quality scores, and win rates — not just revenue and EBITDA.
4. **Operationalize with product-led ownership.** Hire a Senior AI Product Manager as a single point of accountability for prioritization, consolidation, and measurement.

This has strategic implications: AI should help shift toward recurring revenue — alerts, ongoing intelligence, predictive triggers — not just make one-off projects cheaper to deliver.

## Competitive Landscape

The KOL analytics and HCP engagement space is crowded. Large data/analytics competitors include IQVIA, Komodo Health, Definitive Healthcare, Veeva, Symphony Health, ZS, Clarivate, and Axtria. Niche players include Konectar, H1, Neolytica, AMBIT, KOL Insights, and Within3.

That integration is the moat. But it needs to be reinforced with AI, not eroded by tool proliferation.

## Industry Context

AI in pharma commercialization is moving from pilot mode to scaled deployment. The market is projected to grow from ~$1.9B (2025) to $16B+ by 2034. The defining trend for 2026 is the "agentic shift" — AI moving from insight delivery to action execution within workflows. Companies that treat AI as a workflow participant, not just an analysis tool, will set the pace. MKG's editorial pipeline tools are well-positioned for this. The client-facing products need stronger data-driven defensibility.

# 6. Super Product Vision: The AI Editorial Platform

## The Problem: Four Tools, One Pipeline

Today, DynAImic Content, Annotation Activation, Compliance Core, and Route Reagent are built and maintained as separate tools — separate codebases, separate user interfaces, separate onboarding. But they all serve the same workflow: content gets created, claims get annotated with citations, content gets checked for regulatory compliance, and content gets validated against brand and style rules before editorial review.

Running these as four separate products creates compounding inefficiency:

**Redundant infrastructure.** Each tool independently manages document ingestion, LLM routing, brand context loading, and user authentication. A unified platform eliminates this duplicated backend work and reduces maintenance overhead by an estimated 30–40% versus four separate codebases.

**Fragmented user experience.** Users move between tools manually. There is no shared state — an annotation flagged by Compliance Core doesn't automatically route back to DynAImic Content for revision. A routing comment verified by Route Reagent doesn't update the annotation layer. Each handoff is a manual export-import cycle that introduces delay and error.

**Competing roadmaps.** Four product backlogs pull from the same pool of engineering time and medical SME attention. Consolidation focuses investment on a single, coherent product roadmap with one owner.

**Weaker market positioning.** Selling four modular tools is harder than selling one platform that handles the full content lifecycle. A unified product positions MKG as the AI-powered editorial backbone for pharma content — not a collection of helper tools.

## Technology Efficiencies from Unification

**Single LLM orchestration layer.** One routing system (ION) managing model selection, context windowing, and prompt management instead of four separate implementations.

**Shared document model.** Content, annotations, flags, and QA results live in one data structure that flows through the pipeline — no manual handoffs.

**Unified brand knowledge base.** Brand rules, style guides, and past review decisions loaded once and available to all stages. Today each tool loads and manages this context independently.

**One integration surface.** Customers and internal teams connect to one API and one UI, not four separate systems. Reduces onboarding time and support burden.

**Consolidated engineering investment.** Estimated 30–40% reduction in maintenance overhead versus four separate codebases. Frees engineering capacity for feature development instead of infrastructure duplication.

## The Customer Case: Why One Product Beats Four

**Easier adoption.** One onboarding cycle, one integration, one training investment. Pharma brand teams already have tool fatigue — another four modular tools from a single vendor is a hard sell. One platform that handles "brief to MLR-ready" is a fundamentally different conversation.

**Faster cycle times.** Content flows through the full pipeline without manual handoffs between tools. Target: reduce the content-to-MLR cycle from ~14 days to ~8 days on pilot projects.

**Stronger value proposition.** A platform that handles the complete editorial pipeline is infrastructure — it becomes embedded in how work gets done. Four point solutions are optional add-ons that can be cut in a budget review.

**Pricing leverage.** A unified platform commands subscription pricing versus per-tool add-ons. Annual platform licensing creates recurring revenue and higher switching costs.

# 7. Pantheon — HCP Search & Profiling

**What it does.** Pantheon is a conversational AI interface that allows users to query HCP datasets in natural language — for example, "Find HCPs in California publishing on pembrolizumab and receiving payments from BMS in 2023." It provides instant synthesis across publication, clinical trial, and payment data to surface relevant experts in seconds, with integrated visualization and export.

**Who uses it.** Commercial and medical affairs teams at pharma companies — brand leaders, TLL team leadership, MSL teams, insights/analytics groups. Used during engagement planning, speaker identification, and advisory board design.

**What it replaces.** Manual HCP profiling that currently takes 81qd analysts 4–6 weeks per project ($50K–$75K for a standard identification project). Also competes with IQVIA's platforms, H1 Insights, Definitive Healthcare, and Veeva Compass for the same use case.

**Defensibility: MODERATE-HIGH.** Pantheon's value depends on the data underneath it. The integration of MKG's proprietary engagement data (advisory board participation, speaker bureau history, sentiment signals) with commercially available data (publications, trials, payments) creates a richer HCP profile than standalone data vendors can offer. The natural language query interface is increasingly table-stakes, but the data depth is not. The defensibility risk: if Pantheon relies primarily on commercially available data, competitors with better LLM interfaces will close the gap.

**Value framework mapping:** Pricing Power (subscription revenue model shifts 81qd from transaction-based to recurring) + Speed-to-Close (faster HCP identification compresses client engagement timelines).

**Leading indicators:** Subscription attach rate on eligible customers; queries per user per month; renewal rate at 12 months; competitive win rate on proposals featuring Pantheon.

**Value estimate:** See the AI Opportunity Model spreadsheet (External Revenue tab). Low: 8 customers × $75K = $600K. High: 18 customers × $125K = $2.25M. These are early-stage numbers — the low end requires converting fewer than 10% of eligible 81qd clients to subscription.

**What has to be true:** (1) The proprietary engagement data layer is meaningfully richer than what customers can get from IQVIA or H1 alone. (2) The subscription price point ($75K–$150K) is validated with at least 5 pilot customers. (3) Retention exceeds 75% at renewal — which requires demonstrating ongoing usage, not just initial novelty.

# 8. Plexus — Influence Mapping & Network Analytics

**What it does.** Plexus maps clinical influence networks — identifying not just who the key opinion leaders are, but how they're connected, who influences whom, and where the leverage points are in peer-to-peer communication. It combines clinical, academic, digital, and institutional data to model influence flow across therapeutic areas.

**Who uses it.** 81qd analysts, engagement planning teams, commercial strategy leads at pharma companies. Used for network mapping engagements, peer-to-peer program design, and strategic engagement planning.

**What it replaces.** Manual network analysis that relies on subjective assessments, conference attendance lists, and publication counts. Plexus provides algorithmic influence scoring that surfaces regional and digital opinion leaders that traditional methods miss.

**Defensibility: HIGH.** Influence network modeling on proprietary engagement data is the most defensible analytics asset MKG has. The models incorporate who actually attended advisory boards, who spoke at which events, who influenced prescribing behavior changes — data that is generated by MKG's own operations and cannot be purchased externally. This is the product most anchored in MKG's structural advantage.

**Value framework mapping:** Pricing Power (premium analytics that command higher project values) + Productivity (replaces weeks of manual analysis per engagement).

**Leading indicators:** Number of projects delivered using Plexus data; client-reported time savings on engagement planning; competitive win rate on proposals that feature influence mapping.

**Value estimate:** See the AI Opportunity Model spreadsheet. Internal: 15 influence analysts at $130K with 8–15% uplift = $156K–$293K in productivity. External: 10–20 bundled projects with $25K–$50K AI-enhanced pricing premium = $250K–$1.0M in incremental revenue.

**What has to be true:** (1) AI-enhanced Plexus delivers measurably faster or deeper results than the current workflow. (2) Clients perceive the AI enhancement as valuable enough to accept a 10–15% price premium. (3) Sales teams are trained to articulate the AI-enhanced value proposition in proposals.

# 10. Sentiment Tracker — HCP Sentiment Analysis

**What it does.** NLP-based tracking of HCP belief and sentiment shifts over time. Monitors evolution of HCP perceptions across therapeutic areas, treatment protocols, and brand messaging — drawing from MKG's own engagement activities (advisory boards, speaker bureaus, MSL interactions).

**Who uses it.** 81qd analysts, brand strategy teams, market research leads. Used to monitor therapeutic area sentiment, identify emerging resistance or receptivity to messaging, and inform campaign design.

**What it replaces.** Periodic qualitative research (typically quarterly or semi-annual) that provides point-in-time snapshots. Sentiment Tracker aims for continuous monitoring.

**Defensibility: MODERATE.** NLP-based sentiment analysis is increasingly commoditized — off-the-shelf tools can process text for sentiment at scale. MKG's advantage is the *data source*: longitudinal sentiment data from their own advisory boards, speaker bureaus, and MSL interactions is proprietary and cannot be purchased. The tool's defensibility is entirely dependent on this proprietary data stream. If it's applied to publicly available text (publications, social media), it's undifferentiated. If it's applied to MKG's internal engagement data, it's genuinely unique.

**Value framework mapping:** Speed (faster detection of sentiment shifts enables faster campaign adjustments) + Pricing Power (better-targeted engagement strategies improve conversion on existing client relationships).

**Leading indicators:** Sentiment shift detection lag (how quickly are changes surfaced versus periodic research?); correlation between sentiment signals and downstream campaign adjustments; usage frequency by brand strategy teams; client upsell/renewal rate on accounts where sentiment data informed the strategy.

**Value estimate:** Sentiment Tracker doesn't generate direct product revenue, but it can measurably improve conversion and share-of-wallet on existing client relationships. If sentiment-informed targeting improves upsell and renewal rates even modestly, the math works on MKG's existing revenue base. Approximately $50M of MKG's revenue comes from clients where HCP sentiment data is directly relevant to campaign design and engagement planning. A 0.5–1.5% lift in expansion/renewal on that base yields $250K–$750K in incremental annual revenue. # 17. Leading vs. Lagging Indicators

## The Core Argument

MKG's current approach to measuring AI value is almost entirely lagging — revenue, margin, EBITDA. These metrics tell you how you *did*, not how you're *doing*. For initiatives that are early-stage, lagging metrics will show nothing for 6–12 months, which creates a dangerous vacuum: leadership can't tell whether AI investments are working, so momentum stalls or resources get redirected prematurely.

AI initiatives need leading indicators that show whether adoption is taking hold and value is accruing *before* it shows up in financials. The analogy: financials are like checking the rearview mirror. Leading indicators are like checking the road ahead.

## What to Measure Now (Leading Indicators)

| Category | Indicator | Why It Matters |
|----------|-----------|---------------|
| **Adoption** | Daily/weekly active users per tool | If people aren't using the tools, nothing else matters. This is the single most important early metric. |
| **Adoption** | % of eligible projects using AI-assisted workflow | Measures penetration into actual work patterns, not just tool availability. |
| **Cycle Time** | Days from brief to MLR submission | The editorial pipeline's north star metric. Directly tied to the super product vision. |
| **Cycle Time** | Rounds of revision before approval | If AI pre-checks work, fewer rounds should be needed. |
| **Quality** | First-pass acceptance rate at MLR | If Compliance Core and Route Reagent are effective, more submissions should pass on first review. |
| **Quality** | Error rate in AI-generated annotations | Measures whether Annotation Activation is accurate enough to trust. |
| **Client Signal** | Client NPS / satisfaction on AI-enhanced deliverables | Early signal of willingness to pay for AI-differentiated work. |
| **Client Signal** | Proposal win rate on pitches featuring AI capabilities | Measures whether AI tools are actually helping close deals. |
| **Revenue Signal** | Subscription attach rate (Pantheon) | Leading indicator of the transaction-to-subscription revenue shift. |

## What to Track But Not Steer By (Lagging Indicators)

| Category | Indicator | Expected Timeline |
|----------|-----------|-------------------|
| Financial | Revenue from AI-enhanced engagements | 6–12 months |
| Financial | Gross margin improvement from productivity gains | 6–12 months |
| Financial | EBITDA uplift attributable to AI | 12+ months |
| Strategic | Revenue mix shift (transaction → subscription) | 12–24 months |

## Recommendation

Build an enterprise AI KPI dashboard that tracks the leading indicators weekly. Review in the existing Monthly Senior Leadership meeting. Use a 90-day milestone approach: pick one leading indicator per initiative and set a 90-day target. If it's not moving, re-evaluate the initiative before investing further. This creates a feedback loop that's fast enough to course-correct and visible enough to maintain organizational momentum.

# 19. Roadmap & Final Recommendations

## Phase 1: 0–90 Days — Focus & Instrument

**Consolidate the editorial pipeline.** Designate a single product owner for DynAImic Content, Annotation Activation, Compliance Core, and Route Reagent. Unify the backlog. Begin designing the shared document model and stage-based pipeline UX. This doesn't mean merging codebases overnight — it means one roadmap, one owner, and a shared architectural vision.

**Hire or designate the Senior AI Product Manager.** If a full-time hire takes too long, designate an interim owner from the existing team — but with explicit authority over prioritization.

**Set 90-day adoption targets** for Route Reagent and Annotation Activation — the two editorial pipeline tools closest to production readiness. Target: 30%+ of eligible MedComm projects using at least one tool within 90 days.

## Phase 2: 90–180 Days — Prove & Price

**Measure editorial cycle time reduction.** Target: demonstrate 20%+ improvement on pilot projects comparing AI-assisted vs. traditional workflow.

**Run Pantheon subscription pilots** with 5–10 customers. Validate the $75K–$150K pricing range. Measure queries per user, renewal intent, and competitive comparison.

**Validate PerspectivX persona accuracy** against real market research outcomes. If concept scores correlate with actual performance, this becomes a powerful sales tool. If they don't, the product needs recalibration before scaling.

**Begin building the unified editorial platform UX.** Shared document model, stage-based pipeline interface, customer-specific guardrail configuration.

## Phase 3: 6–12 Months — Scale & Differentiate

**Roll out the unified editorial platform** across all MedComm business units. Target: 60%+ of eligible projects on the platform.

**Scale Pantheon subscriptions** to 15–25 customers. Begin measuring retention at the 12-month mark.

**Introduce the predictive layer for Plexus** — alert system for new publications, conference presentations, and behavior changes; predictive HCP triggers for drug class and diagnosis forecasting. These features drive the transaction-to-subscription shift for 81qd.

**Sunset or deprioritize tools** that haven't demonstrated adoption or value by the 6-month mark. If a tool has fewer than 20% adoption among eligible users after two quarters of availability, it's not solving a real problem.

**Publish the first "AI Impact Report"** for the Novo board. Show leading indicator trends, EBITDA bridge progress, and portfolio rationalization outcomes. This becomes the accountability mechanism for the full AI investment.

## The Four Cards

**1. Rationalize the portfolio.** Consolidate adjacent products. Stop building net-new until overlap is addressed. One strong platform beats nine mediocre tools.

**2. Differentiate through data + expertise.** The moat is proprietary engagement data, two decades of medical judgment, and integrated commercialization services — not AI capability. Every product should be evaluated against the question: "Does this use data or expertise that a competitor cannot buy?"

**3. Instrument ROI.** Leading indicators first. Close the loop between product usage → outcomes → willingness to pay. Even partial measurement transforms the conversation.

**4. Operationalize delivery.** Product-led prioritization with clear ownership, a single intake process, and 90-day milestones. Pick the most tractable win, not the most valuable one. Build momentum through demonstrated results.
