## Slide 2 — Two Booms That Shape These Lessons

**Visual concept:** Two vertical panels, each with a large watercolor-style abstract wave form behind the text — one cresting and crashing (health tech), one still rising with no visible peak (AI). The visual tells the story: one boom completed its cycle, the other hasn't.

**Panel 1 — The Health Tech Boom**
- Massive capital. Strong talent. Good products built.
- Valuations inflated beyond what business models could support
- Digital therapeutics: no reimbursement pathway
- Value-based care: only addressed cost
- AI biotech: didn't accelerate clinical timelines
- **The boom burst because the commercial models hadn't been worked out — not because the technology failed**

**Panel 2 — The AI Boom**
- Technology advancing faster than companies can adopt
- Entire categories opening up, then getting consumed by foundation models
- Companies that engage: accelerating
- Companies on the periphery: compounding uncertainty
- **The defining feature isn't the technology — it's the pace**

**Bottom bar:**
Both booms rewarded companies that connected innovation to commercial reality early. Both punished companies that confused technical progress with business value.

## Slide 3 — The Exponential Trajectory of AI

**Visual concept:** Horizontal timeline with an exponential curve overlaid — flat on the left, hockey-sticking to the right. Each era is a labeled zone along the curve. The curve itself communicates the acceleration better than any text could. Below the curve, a single row showing "Enterprise ability to absorb" as a much flatter, barely rising line — visualizing the gap.

**Era 1 — Pre-2017: Custom builds**
- Bespoke development for every application
- Long timelines, limited reusability

**Era 2 — 2017–2020: Reusable models**
- Deep learning tools accessible
- Research-to-tool cycle compressing
- Still required significant custom engineering

**Era 3 — 2021–2024: Foundation models**
- General-purpose models handle broad enterprise tasks
- Cost of AI-enabled workflows dropped by orders of magnitude
- Startups flooded the market

**Era 4 — 2025+: Agents and automation**
- AI systems that take actions autonomously
- The question shifted from "does it work?" to "can we absorb the change?"

**Annotation on the gap between curves:**
The external market is innovating faster than any internal team can. This gap is the core strategic problem.

## Slide 4 — What Worked and What Didn't

**Visual concept:** A simple ledger — left side green, right side red, like a balance sheet of innovation outcomes. Each entry is a one-line headline with a short qualifier underneath. Not paragraphs — entries. The format signals judgment: these are settled conclusions, not open questions.

**What Worked (green side):**

**Direct-to-patient marketing platforms**
Fueled the core business. Adapted consumer digital to medical context. Generated value the org already knew how to measure.

**B2B customer-facing platform in pharma services**
Fed core revenue. Leadership tracked success in familiar terms. Innovation tied to the commercial engine gets oxygen.

**Properly structured external investments**
Equity where outside investors participate. Value on market terms. Independent governance. Room to prove themselves.

**What Didn't (red side):**

**Digital therapeutics**
Full clinical development. No reimbursement pathway on the other side. Approval ≠ revenue.

**Purpose-built health AI platforms**
Overtaken by foundation models improving through scale. Narrow moats get eaten.

**Products without measurement infrastructure**
Couldn't prove value. Couldn't justify funding. Didn't fail — couldn't prove it succeeded.

**Venture studios with misaligned incentives**
Equity treated as discounted consulting. Economics favored fees. Structure shapes behavior — always.

**Bottom bar:**
Innovation survives when it connects to the core commercial engine. External investments need external structures.

## Slide 5 — The Lessons

**Visual concept:** A single column of nine numbered statements, each in its own horizontal bar. Left-aligned numbers in sage circles. Clean, definitive, no decoration. This is the reference page — it should feel like a manifesto pinned to a wall. Consider alternating subtle background shading (white/sage[50]) for readability.

1. Start with business value and let the case strengthen through evidence
2. Look outside first — partner, rent, or build, but know that the best technology is often already in the market
3. Move fast — close the deal, do the work, sort out complex terms while making progress
4. Set up simple KPIs to measure and realize success early — this may cost money, but without evidence you can't justify continued investment
5. Run fewer initiatives with faster proof and clearer ownership
6. Treat external investments with external structures — clear strategic posture, equity where outside investors can participate, independent governance
7. Don't put P&L leaders on top of innovation that takes years to realize value
8. Build cross-functional teams with outside talent and shared processes and vocabulary
9. Treat innovation like a lean transformation — continuous improvement, not a one-time win

**Footer (italics):**
This is an operations problem with amazing technology at your fingertips and change that needs to happen nonlinearly in order to win.

## Slide 6 — Start With Business Value

**Visual concept:** A funnel or filter diagram. At the top: a wide opening labeled "all possible initiatives." Each question below acts as a filter that narrows the funnel. At the bottom: a narrow output labeled "initiatives worth funding." The visual communicates that most ideas should get filtered out — the framework is reductive by design.

**Section label:** Lesson 1

**Filter 1 — What moves the business?**
Revenue. Margin. Cycle time. Quality. Risk reduction. If it takes a paragraph to explain why it matters, it probably doesn't yet.

**Filter 2 — Where can technology realize impact fastest?**
Start where the workflow exists and the pain is measurable. The most successful early initiatives are unglamorous — automating a manual step, accelerating a review, improving data access. These build credibility for harder changes later.

**Filter 3 — Can you measure progress in 90 days?**
Establish a baseline. Show movement. If you can't, you won't hold organizational attention. Measuring may require infrastructure investment — that's part of the cost, not a separate fight.

**Filter 4 — Does the case need to be perfect or improve?**
Most initial business cases are wrong. Fund based on evidence of improvement, not precision of prediction. A locked five-year case either kills good ideas early or funds bad ones too long.

## Slide 7 — Look Outside First: Partner, Rent, or Build

**Visual concept:** A three-lane decision sort. At the top, a single entry point: "New capability needed." Three lanes flow downward, each a different width — Partner lane is widest (this is the default), Build lane is medium, Danger Zone is narrow but visually prominent (amber/warning treatment). The visual communicates that partnering should be the default path, building is newly viable but scoped, and the middle ground is where companies get stuck.

**Section label:** Lessons 2 & 3

**The default: Partner**
The capital booms funded enormous amounts of good technology. It already exists. For complex, domain-specific capabilities requiring sustained R&D, the external market is better and faster. Rent access. Don't rebuild what the ecosystem already offers.

- Speed matters more than terms. 9–12 months evaluating and negotiating = 9–12 months of lost progress.
- Startups don't operate on a balance sheet. They're running out of time while you deliberate.
- Pay them like a dev shop. Start learning. Walk away if it doesn't work. Rounding error downside.
- Stop agonizing about IP — it's not a molecule. Technology can be engineered around. Sort out complex rights while doing work together, not before.

**The new option: Build simple things yourself**
AI has changed the build calculus. Teams can now prototype in days what used to take months. Many SaaS products that charged enterprise prices can be replaced with a well-prompted model and a simple wrapper. Don't pay for what you can build in a day.

- Internal tools, workflow automation, simple integrations — just build them
- LLM wrapper + high-performing general model can now replace entire product categories
- If it's simple and internal, the fastest path is often doing it yourself

**The danger zone: Prototype ≠ product**
Easy to demo. Hard to make enterprise-grade. Security, compliance, reliability, integration, maintenance. Companies build a prototype in a week and spend 18 months trying to productionize it.

- Know when you've crossed from "we can handle this" to "we need a partner"
- The trap is the middle ground — looks easy to build, hard to maintain at scale
- Don't mistake a working demo for a production system

**Bottom bar:**
Look outside first for quality and speed. Build when it's simple. Partner when it's complex. Recognize the danger zone in between.

## Slide 8 — Set Up Simple KPIs and Invest in Measuring Them

**Visual concept:** A split dashboard mockup — left half is a live, populated dashboard with green/amber indicators and trend lines. Right half is the same dashboard but grayed out, with "no data" and "last updated: 6 months ago" labels. The visual shows what measurement looks like when invested in vs. neglected. The contrast tells the story.

**Section label:** Lesson 4

**Why this matters**
- The obvious miss: funding something that isn't working
- The bigger miss: something IS working and nobody sees it — so you fail to double down at the moment it matters most
- Measurement enables decisive action in both directions

**Establish KPIs before you start**
Define success before work begins. Forces clarity on what the initiative is supposed to accomplish.

**Keep the list short**
Companies routinely define 20+. That's a wish list, not a measurement plan. Three to five metrics. Commit.

**Make them relevant to the people doing the work**
A KPI only works if the person looking at it can affect it. Adoption ≠ value. How often people log in is not a business outcome.

**Invest in the infrastructure**
Dashboards don't populate themselves. Data feeds, integrations, possibly paid sources. Costs money. Takes iteration. Part of the cost of the initiative.

**Invest in the process to respond**
Right people. Regular cadence. Authority to act. Measurement without a response loop is just reporting.

## Slide 9 — Fewer Initiatives, Faster Proof, Clearer Ownership

**Visual concept:** A before/after portfolio map. Left side ("Before"): a chaotic scatter of 15–20 small dots in various colors, unlabeled, spread across the slide — representing a fragmented, unrationalized portfolio. Right side ("After"): 4 larger dots, each labeled, connected by clear lines to business outcomes at the bottom. Some of the original dots have X marks through them. The visual communicates: rationalization means cutting, combining, and connecting what remains to value.

**Section label:** Lesson 5

**Every company has an AI roadmap. Most have too much on it.**
Table stakes to have AI in the executive deck. But the roadmap is usually a collection of accumulated initiatives — some top-down, many bottom-up, not all tied to business value.

**Too decentralized to succeed**
Names attached, but no cross-functional structure. Similar efforts in parallel across groups. Nobody looking across the portfolio for overlap or gaps.

**Rationalize ruthlessly**
Combine some. Cut many. A few get real investment, teams, and focus.

**Stop running pilots**
"Pilot" = dipping a toe in. Instead: "We are working in AI now. This is our first step. We will iterate and build." Commitment, not evaluation.

**Use measurement to steer**
KPIs tell you whether to stay the course, double down, shift, or cut. The portfolio sharpens through response to evidence, not better planning.

## Slide 10 — External Structures for External Investments

**Visual concept:** Five distinct "posture cards" arranged in a spectrum from fully external (left) to fully internal (right). Each card has a different visual weight or color intensity — lightest on the left (VC-style, most independent) to darkest on the right (internal, most integrated). A dotted line underneath connects them, with labels showing the spectrum: "Most independent → Most integrated." The layout communicates that these are positions on a continuum, not interchangeable options.

**Section label:** Lesson 6

**Heading:** The language around corporate investment gets muddied. These are different postures. Pick one and build accordingly.

**Invest like a VC**
Equity value creation. Financial return. Requires venture-grade governance, independent decisions, investor-style incentives. If the team isn't compensated like investors, they won't act like investors.

**Invest like a strategic partner**
Act as a customer with preferred rights and access. Value = what the partnership delivers to the core business. Equity is secondary.

**Invest like a strategic acquirer**
Option to buy with a front-line view. Paying for proximity, information, and optionality. Not near-term returns.

**Externalize but own**
Separate governance, team, and P&L. Operates on its own cycles and decision speed. Revenue should look like something — new, or even competing with the core. Hardest posture. Requires tolerating real independence.

**Innovate inside the business**
Must feed the core P&L or it will die. Don't lie to yourself about value creation.

**Bottom bar:**
The failure is almost never picking the wrong posture. It's not picking one clearly.

## Slide 11 — Leadership and Resourcing

**Visual concept:** A "role anatomy" diagram — a single box representing the innovation leader role, with four diagnostic layers radiating outward like rings or an organizational x-ray. Each ring represents a requirement: Appetite for risk (innermost), Real budget and team, Defined lane vs IT, Top-down commitment from above (outermost). Rings that are complete = healthy. Missing rings = role is set up to fail. The visual communicates that the leader's success depends on the layers around them, not just who they are.

**Section label:** Lesson 7

**Right leader, wrong setup = failure**
Senior execs take innovation roles when their path upward is blocked. They want something new but default to familiar politics and value structures. The program mirrors the culture it should challenge.

**Title without resources = designed to fail**
The health tech boom: dozens of VP Digital Health roles, two reports, no budget. Now repeating with Chief AI Officer titles. Expected to succeed through influence. They don't. They get blamed. But the setup was wrong.

**AI leadership needs a defined lane**
Adjacent to IT without clear boundaries = constant friction. Define what the role owns, what it influences, where IT is partner vs gatekeeper.

**Top-down commitment, not tool distribution**
Enterprise AI license + login count ≠ transformation. Leadership drives direction, resources, obstacles, and accountability for value — not activity.

## Slide 12 — Team Composition

**Visual concept:** A team assembly diagram — like a puzzle or Venn diagram showing four overlapping talent pools: Internal operators (org knowledge, credibility), External builders (startup instincts, speed), Technical specialists (AI/ML depth, expensive), Cross-functional connectors (shared vocabulary, process). The center where they overlap is labeled "Innovation team that works." The visual communicates that you need all four, not just one.

**Section label:** Lesson 8

**Cross-functional is required**
Technology, operations, commercial, customer experience — cannot be run by a single function.

**Internal talent alone is not enough**
Insiders know the org. Outsiders know how to build. Startup experience, technical depth, design thinking bring different instincts — faster iteration, less consensus-seeking, more comfort with ambiguity. Need both.

**Technical talent is expensive. Plan for it.**
AI companies pay enormous compensation. That's the market. Underpaying gets you either no talent or wrong-tier talent. Both slow everything down.

**Shared vocabulary and processes early**
Designers, strategists, engineers, regulatory leads — literally use different words. Without defined standards for communication and decision-making, teams churn on process instead of making progress.

## Slide 13 — Lean Transformation, Not a One-Time Win

**Visual concept:** A compounding improvement curve. X-axis: time (quarters or years). Y-axis: capability/value. Two lines plotted. Line 1 ("One-time deployment"): a single step-up that then flatlines. Line 2 ("Continuous reinvestment"): a staircase that keeps climbing, each step building on the last, with the gap between the two lines widening dramatically over time. Annotate the gap: "This is the value left on the table." The visual makes the compounding argument impossible to ignore.

**Section label:** Lesson 9

**Innovation in AI is an operations problem**
Treat it like a lean transformation. Continuous improvement. Understand the work. Change the work. Operational discipline applied to extraordinary technology.

**Commit to a new way of working**
Pilot = end date. Transformation = ongoing. Iterate, measure, reinvest. Companies that treat this as bounded capture a fraction of the value and stall.

**Focus on jobs to be done, not headcount**
The unit of analysis is the work itself. What's being done, how, what changes with better tools. Reframes AI from cost reduction to capability transformation.

**The improvement is nonlinear**
Not 10–20%. Hundreds or thousands of percent — but only through sustained reinvestment. Implement, learn, adjust, reinvest. Each cycle compounds. One-time deployment = one-time improvement. Continuous commitment = transformation.

**Same answer for operators and investors**
"What productivity improvement should we expect?" Wrong question. The value evolves over time when done right. Not a slug to model in a spreadsheet. A system that gets better.

## Slide 14 — Signs of Success and Signs of Risk

**Visual concept:** Two columns styled like overheard conversations — speech bubbles or quote blocks. Left column (green tint) reads like things you'd hear walking through a company that's getting it right. Right column (red tint) reads like things you'd hear in a company that's not. No analysis, no explanation — just the quotes. The reader does the pattern matching themselves. Consider a subtle "overheard in the hallway" header treatment to set the frame.

**Left column — "If you hear things like this, it's going well"**

"We cut the roadmap from 15 initiatives to four — here's why these four connect to pipeline acceleration and margin improvement."

"We signed a paid pilot with that computational biology startup six weeks after the first call — legal is sorting out the long-term IP terms while we're already generating data together."

"We hired a former startup CTO to run the innovation team alongside our clinical ops lead and commercial strategist — they run weekly stand-ups and make decisions in the room."

"Part of our investment strategy was contingent on being the first paying customer — it gave us preferred access to the roadmap and helped them close their next round with outside investors."

"Our medical writing tool is in its third iteration — each version handles more document types and we track time savings per submission against the baseline we set on day one."

"The team had been pushing hard on two initiatives with disciplined KPI tracking. The data made it clear the fit wasn't there. We celebrated the learning, moved the talent and budget to initiatives with better traction, and we're already seeing results. That's the system working."

"Our startup partner shipped a new feature last week that we asked for — they're moving faster than our internal teams ever could."

"Our investment has already doubled in equity value — outside investors came in at a higher mark in the last round. The company is still pre-profitability but that's expected at this stage. We're not holding it to a P&L standard."

"We're spending real money on the data infrastructure to measure this — it wasn't cheap but now we actually know what's working."

"We stopped calling them pilots — these are committed operating changes with dedicated teams and quarterly reviews."

"Our innovation budget has its own governance — it doesn't compete with the core P&L for annual approval. The funding cycle matches the innovation lifecycle, not the budget calendar. Some of these will take multiple years to realize full value and we've structured for that."

**Right column — "If you hear things like this, something's wrong"**

"We have a fully documented AI strategy with a roadmap of 20 initiatives and an Excel tracker with owners, timelines, and dependencies for each one."

"72% of our employees are using ChatGPT on a weekly basis — we're really seeing strong adoption across the organization."

"We're still making progress on the partnership with that clinical trial company — just waiting on MLR review for the last six weeks, not sure where it's at."

"We just hired a VP of AI Innovation — still sorting out what their reporting structure will be, but this person seems super connected."

"We've got the dashboard but it hasn't been updated — the person in charge of that rotated out and we haven't invested in the automated feeds yet."

"We invested in that digital therapeutics company but the terms are complicated — they're having trouble raising their next round."

"Three teams are piloting document summarization tools — great to see the enthusiasm."

"We set up a venture arm last year — we've made eight investments but I couldn't tell you which ones are strategic and which ones are financial."

"We tried an AI tool for clinical document review but it wasn't perfect so we went back to the manual process."

"Our board wants to see the five-year ROI case before we commit any more funding."

"Everyone's excited about AI — we're letting teams experiment and see what bubbles up."

**Bottom bar:**
These patterns are visible in almost every large company running an innovation program. The specifics vary. The structural issues don't.
